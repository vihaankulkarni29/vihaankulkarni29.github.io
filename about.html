My journey from biology to code, and why I believe computational approaches are critical for solving the antimicrobial resistance crisis.

That experience sparked a realization—the next major breakthroughs in biology won't come from better microscopes or faster sequencers alone. This drove me to learn programming, pipeline development, and machine learning, always with one goal: making computational biology accessible and practical for working researchers.

Why Antimicrobial Resistance Matters

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is projected to cause 10 million deaths annually by 2050, surpassing cancer as a leading cause of mortality. Yet our ability to track and understand resistance mechanisms lags far behind the pace of bacterial evolution.

The core problem: Traditional microbiological approaches take days to weeks to identify resistance patterns. By the time results arrive, infections have progressed, inappropriate antibiotics have been prescribed, and resistance has potentially spread to new patients.

Computational genomics offers a path forward. By analyzing bacterial genomes, we can:

  • Identify resistance genes in hours instead of days
  • Track the spread of resistant strains across hospitals and regions
  • Predict which mutations are likely to emerge next
  • Design targeted interventions before outbreaks become crises

The Democratization Challenge

The bottleneck isn't sequencing capacity—it's analysis. Many clinical labs and research institutions in low- and middle-income countries have access to genomic data but lack the computational infrastructure or expertise to analyze it effectively. My work focuses on building tools that bridge this gap: simple, robust, well-documented software that doesn't require a computer science degree to use.

If we can democratize access to these analytical capabilities, we can enable a truly global surveillance network for antimicrobial resistance. That's the vision driving my work.

Development Philosophy

When building bioinformatics tools, I follow several core principles:

1. Reproducibility Over Novelty

I prioritize tools that produce consistent, reproducible results over cutting-edge methods that work only in ideal conditions. If researchers can't trust the output, the tool is useless regardless of how sophisticated the algorithm is.

2. Documentation Is Features

A poorly documented tool is a broken tool. Every project includes comprehensive documentation, example workflows, and troubleshooting guides. If users need to email me to understand how to use the software, I've failed.

3. Open Source Always

Science advances through collaboration and transparency. All my tools are open source, allowing researchers to inspect, modify, and improve the code. Proprietary bioinformatics software slows down research and creates unnecessary barriers.

4. Real-World Testing

Every tool is developed in collaboration with researchers who will actually use it. I don't build solutions in search of problems—I solve problems that are actively blocking research.
